Survival analysis is a statistical method used to analyze the time until a significant event occurs—ranging from patient mortality and mechanical failure to customer churn and business duration. Parametric survival models remain popular due to their ability to estimate both relative (hazard ratio) and absolute effects (survival probability), and their capacity for extrapolation. However, the accuracy of these models critically depends on selecting the correct survival distribution, which is often challenged by censored data and the mismatch between theoretical hazard assumptions and real-world patterns.

Traditional methods for identifying survival distributions—such as the Likelihood Ratio Test (LRT), graphical methods, and AIC/BIC—have notable limitations. LRTs are only applicable to nested models and are sensitive to high censoring. Graphical approaches are subjective, while AIC/BIC may overfit large or heavily censored datasets. Meanwhile, recent advances in Large Language Models (LLMs) such as GPT-4, BERT, and LLaMA have shown promise in tackling complex statistical problems, including survival prediction, especially when combining structured and unstructured clinical data.

Despite their potential, no existing approach has utilized LLMs specifically for identifying survival distributions and generating human-readable statistical interpretations. This project addresses that gap by leveraging GPT-4o mini with fine-tuning and prompt engineering techniques to build an interactive web-based platform. The platform integrates LLM capabilities with traditional statistical tools, providing an accessible solution for users with limited technical backgrounds—bridging the gap between complex survival modeling and user-friendly analysis.
